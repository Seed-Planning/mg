Messages to Share

Insights and feelings that emerged through lived experience

Diagnosis, treatment, and life itself have all left patients with thoughts they want to share.
Here we introduce words born from experience—messages they hope others living with the same condition, and the people around them, will hear.

The strength of connection

Many patients described a strong sense of loneliness and anxiety when they were first diagnosed. They said that connecting with others who live with the same condition through social media helped them feel that they were not alone. Some also felt hesitant or anxious about meeting people directly, but many said that having a place where they could share information and emotions while keeping a comfortable distance became an important source of emotional support.

What they want others to know

Messages rooted in experience were shared again and again: “Please do not give up even if antibodies are not detected,” “Tell your doctor whenever something feels off,” and “I hope you can find a medical institution and a person you can talk to.” Many patients felt that understanding support systems and public resources, and allowing themselves to rely on help instead of pushing through alone, is essential to living with the condition over time.

Patient Voices

At the time of diagnosis, she felt lonely because she had no opportunity to connect with others living with the same condition. She says that now, through social media and patient groups, she has found support through connection. She wants to tell others that there will always be someone else living with the same illness, and that she hopes they will not carry everything alone but find someone they can rely on.

Patient A (Female, 20s)

He made up his mind to live as a person with a disability and has built his life while researching the systems and support available to him. Because social media contains a great deal of misinformation about public support, he has actively responded when others ask him questions, wanting to share accurate information. He wants people to know that they do not have to give up in despair, that it is important to find someone to consult, and that life can be sustained by making use of available support systems.

Patient B (Male, 50s)

She feels that even symptoms people dismiss as “just tiredness” can lead to diagnosis if they are shared with a doctor. She also wants others to know how important it is to find a doctor and medical institution that suit them, because the fit between patient and healthcare professional can greatly influence the course of treatment. Connecting with others who have the same illness through social media and being able to complain openly with them became a major source of support for her,… while at the same time she strongly felt the need for a place where information about support systems and assistive devices could be gathered in one accessible form.

Patient C (Female, 30s)

She says that seeing social media posts from others with the same illness helped her feel that she was not alone, and that this became an important source of emotional support. Although she still feels unsure about meeting people in person, she hopes there can be spaces where people can participate with a sense of safety at each stage. She also looks back and says she wishes she had known in advance that examinations and treatment are not always as frightening as the most alarming information makes them seem,… and she hopes understanding of illnesses that cannot be seen will spread more widely through society.

Patient D (Female, 40s)

Although she felt deeply pessimistic at the time of diagnosis, she says she eventually came to feel that calmer days do arrive. Connecting with others who have the same illness on X and being able to share information about treatment and surgery became a major source of support for her. She wants others to know that they do not have to carry everything alone, that it can help to talk with people who live with the same condition, and that even anonymous spaces online can matter.

Patient E (Female, 30s)

It took time for her to accept the illness, but she says that now she values not pushing herself, relying on people she can rely on, and allowing herself not to do what she cannot do. She feels that not exceeding her own capacity, and allowing herself to rest without guilt, are essential to living with the condition over the long term—and she wants others with the same illness to know that too.

Patient F (Female, 30s)

Because he went so long without a diagnosis, he wants others to know the importance of looking for another medical institution and seeking more than one opinion. Having been helped by other patients’ experiences and hospital information shared on X, he also feels strongly that spaces where people with the same illness can connect are very important.

Navigating Medical Care

Living with symptoms that are hard to see can place a far greater burden on a person than many imagine.
Even when someone has been diagnosed with a designated intractable disease, it can still be difficult for others to understand
because the condition is not outwardly visible and symptoms may vary even within a single day.

Patients also spoke about holding back or hesitating, and being unable to share how they truly felt.Today, more people are speaking about their illness, and opportunities to connect with others living with the same condition are growing.

Even so, understanding across society as a whole has not yet spread far enough.
Because these symptoms are hard to see, patients still often struggle to be understood by those around them.
Taking a moment to notice the daily lives of people who may be near us.
That is the first step toward understanding.
