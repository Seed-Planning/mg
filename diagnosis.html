Challenges in Daily Life

Symptoms that are hard to see, and the adjustments people make in daily life

The symptoms of myasthenia gravis can be difficult to recognize from the outside, and there are times when daily life requires adjustments and practical coping strategies.
Here we share some of the challenges patients face in everyday life, and the ways they adapt in response to how they are feeling.

Invisible symptoms and
daily fluctuation

Because the symptoms of myasthenia gravis can be hard to see from the outside, some patients felt that it was difficult for those around them to understand what they were going through. Symptoms may worsen in the evening or vary greatly from day to day, making it necessary to adjust work, housework, and plans outside the home with flexibility.

Each person’s own ways of coping

Patients spoke of specific difficulties such as reduced walking distance, increased trouble with stairs and fine motor tasks, or being unable to keep their arms raised for long. Some felt reluctant to use a cane or assistive devices, yet accepted them in order to conserve energy. In daily life, they reduce the burden in their own ways—adapting cooking tools and household items, doing things earlier in the day, and choosing not to push themselves too hard.

Emotional burden

At the same time, patients also spoke about how being misunderstood, or being hurt by insensitive comments, can become a significant emotional burden when the illness is not visible to others.

Patient Voices

Her symptoms currently worsen from the evening onward, and there are many days when heaviness in her arms and legs forces her to lie down. Side effects from injection treatment sometimes make her change plans at short notice, and she feels that daily life always requires adjustment. She does housework during the times of day when she feels better, and after evening relies on her family, choosing not to push herself.… At the same time, she looks back on moments when others found it difficult to understand what she was going through because her symptoms were hard to see from the outside.

Patient A (Female, 20s)

Double vision, reduced motor function, and high blood sugar have made moving around and carrying out everyday activities a major burden. He says that even now he spends more than half of each day lying down, and that his level of activity is limited. To conserve energy, he makes a point of deciding what not to do, and manages daily life with the help of assistive devices and a power wheelchair.

Patient B (Male, 50s)

Even now, the effects of medication fluctuate, and by evening she cannot raise her arms, making housework difficult; as a result, she needs to plan her whole day earlier than before. Because she cannot drive, getting around in rural areas is also a major burden. Since her condition is not visible, she has been hurt by comments like, “You’re young, so if you try hard enough you’ll get better,” and she says that she has also struggled with insensitive reactions to… changes in her appearance caused by treatment.

Patient C (Female, 30s)

She still experiences double vision, and feels anxious about fine tasks and moving through places with steps or uneven surfaces. She says she sometimes cuts her fingers while clipping her nails or cooking, and when she goes out, she adjusts by relying on the field of vision available through one eye. Because her symptoms are hard to see, even when she wears a Help Mark badge, she feels that others rarely understand her situation, and that people do not grasp the unsteadiness or visual instability she lives with.

Patient D (Female, 40s)

She still experiences heaviness in her hands and becomes easily tired, so actions like using a hair dryer or writing can be burdensome. Some days she feels short of breath, and she says she does housework little by little while resting according to how she feels. Because her condition is not visible, she is often told things like, “It’s a disease that can be cured, isn’t it?” and feels that even when she explains it, people do not truly understand. She adds that steroid…-related changes in body shape and lowered immunity are also among the difficulties of daily life.

Patient E (Female, 30s)

Her walking distance has decreased considerably, and going out alone has become difficult. She says that symptoms such as drooping eyelids, double vision, and shortness of breath are hard to see from the outside, making them difficult to convey to others. At first she was reluctant to use a cane, but once she realized it made things easier, she came to accept choosing not to push herself. She now conserves energy by… finding ways to adapt cooking tools and everyday items.

Patient F (Female, 30s)

To avoid fatigue, he tries to live in ways that conserve his strength—using stairs as little as possible, taking a cane on long outings, and adjusting how he brushes his teeth or uses a hair dryer so that he does not have to keep his arms raised for long. Because it is a condition that is hard to see, he says that after he started wearing a Help Mark badge, he also experienced insensitive remarks from others and became very aware of how easily people misunderstand.
