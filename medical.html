Living Each Day with
“Invisible Symptoms”

Voices of People Living with Myasthenia Gravis

Myasthenia gravis is a designated intractable disease in Japan. Many of its symptoms are difficult to see from the outside, and for some people it can take time to reach a diagnosis.
Symptoms also tend to change depending on the time of day and physical condition, and there are many moments when others may not fully understand.
This “invisibility” can affect not only daily life,
but emotional well-being as well.

For this website, we spoke with seven people living with myasthenia gravis and gathered their experiences—from the journey to diagnosis and the challenges of daily life,
to their relationships with medical care and the reflections that have emerged through living with the condition.

Journey to Diagnosis

From subtle early signs to finally receiving a diagnosis

The early signs of myasthenia gravis can be difficult to recognize, and reaching a diagnosis may take time.
Here we share patients’ accounts of the first sense that something was wrong, and the uncertainty and hesitation they experienced before diagnosis.

  • I thought it was just fatigue
  • Even after visiting several departments, I still had no diagnosis
  • My antibodies were not detected, and a psychological cause was suspected
  • Finding the words “designated intractable disease” online made my anxiety grow

Challenges in Daily Life

Living with symptom fluctuation while finding ways to cope

Because the symptoms are difficult to see from the outside, daily challenges can be hard to explain to others.
Here we share the realities of symptom fluctuation, everyday coping strategies, and the emotional weight that can come with them.

  • Symptoms worsen in the evening, and can vary greatly from day to day
  • Walking, stairs, and fine motor tasks have become more difficult
  • It can be hard to choose not to push oneself
  • Not being understood can become an emotional burden

Navigating Medical Care

Treatment decisions and relationships with healthcare professionals

There is no single treatment path, and people may need to make choices based on their lives and values.
Here we share voices about treatment decisions and relationships with healthcare professionals.

  • Choosing while weighing effectiveness, side effects, cost, and the impact on daily life
  • Clear explanations and ongoing dialogue can bring reassurance
  • Organizing information during times of intense anxiety
  • Relationships with primary physicians and nurses

Messages to Share

The power of connection, and what patients hope others will know

Meeting others who live with the same condition, and having a place where one can talk with peace of mind, can be a powerful source of support.
Here we share the messages patients most want others to hear.

  • Social media and communities helped me feel I was not alone
  • Finding the right medical institution and support contacts matters
  • It helps to know about support systems and public resources
  • Do not push yourself too hard—create places and relationships you can rely on
